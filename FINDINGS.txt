────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. WHAT THE PROJECT IS ABOUT
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project takes the theoretical algorithms from the paper "The Longest
Common Bitonic Subsequence: A Match-Sensitive Dynamic Programming Approach"
by Rahat and Hasan (2026) and implements them in Python from scratch, then
runs them on real biological data to see how well the theoretical claims
hold in practice.

A bitonic sequence is one that strictly increases up to a single peak value
and then strictly decreases. For example: 1, 3, 6, 4 is bitonic — it rises
to 6, then falls.

The Longest Common Bitonic Subsequence (LCBS) of two sequences A and B is
the longest bitonic sequence that appears as a subsequence in both A and B.

In biology, gene expression profiles over time often follow this pattern:
a gene is activated (expression rises), reaches peak activity, then is
repressed (expression falls). The LCBS between two genes captures how long
their shared rise-and-fall pattern is.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. METHODS — WHAT WE IMPLEMENTED
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We implemented two exact algorithms from the paper:

ALGORITHM 1 — Baseline (Θ(nm))
  - Runs a row-scan LCIS algorithm twice: once forward, once on the
    reversed sequences.
  - The forward pass computes INC(i,j) — the length of the longest common
    increasing subsequence ending at match (i,j).
  - The backward pass on reversed input computes DEC(i,j) — the length
    of the longest common decreasing subsequence starting at (i,j).
  - The peak is the match (i,j) that maximises INC(i,j) + DEC(i,j) - 1.
  - Reconstruction follows stored predecessor and successor pointers.
  - Time complexity: Theta(nm) — always quadratic, regardless of input.

ALGORITHM 2 — Match-Sensitive (O(M log²M))
  - Only operates on the M matching pairs (i,j) where A[i] = B[j].
  - Uses coordinate compression to rank j-indices and values.
  - Uses a sparse 2D Binary Indexed Tree (Fenwick tree) to answer
    dominance range-maximum queries in O(log²M) per match.
  - Forward pass computes INC using ascending row order.
  - Backward pass computes DEC using mirrored j-rank: x_hat = J - rJ + 1,
    which converts a suffix-in-j query into a prefix query the BIT handles.
  - Time complexity: O(M log²M) — depends on M, the number of matches,
    not on the full grid nm.

CORRECTNESS FINDING DURING IMPLEMENTATION:
  We discovered a bug in a naive implementation of Algorithm 2. If two
  matches (i, j1) and (i, j2) share the same row i with j1 < j2, and
  (i, j1) is inserted into the BIT before (i, j2) is queried, then (i,j1)
  falsely appears to dominate (i,j2). Strict dominance requires i' < i,
  not i' <= i. This produced non-bitonic output sequences in testing.
  Fix: all BIT queries in a row must complete before any updates in that
  same row. After this fix, 500/500 random test pairs returned valid
  bitonic common subsequences.

DATA PIPELINE — GSE3431:
  - Downloaded NCBI GEO series matrix: 9,335 probe sets x 36 time points.
  - Applied quantile normalisation across all 36 samples.
  - Fitted a piecewise-linear bitonic model to each gene's expression
    profile, trying all possible peak positions. Kept genes with R² >= 0.25.
  - Discretised each 36-point profile into integer bins {0,...,9} using
    alpha = 10 quantile bins with jitter for tie-breaking.
  - Result: 3,785 bitonic genes available for LCBS analysis.
  - Selected top 100 genes by R² score, formed 500 gene pairs, ran both
    algorithms on each pair.


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. RESULTS — WHAT WE FOUND
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

SYNTHETIC BENCHMARKS (n x alpha grid, 10 trials each):

  n=1000, alpha=10:
    Baseline:        211 ms
    Match-Sensitive: 660 ms
    M (matches):     ~100,000
    Speedup:         0.32x  (baseline is faster)

  n=20, alpha=50:
    Baseline:        0.044 ms
    Match-Sensitive: 0.033 ms
    M (matches):     ~8
    Speedup:         1.35x  (match-sensitive is faster)

  n=50, alpha=50:
    Baseline:        0.263 ms
    Match-Sensitive: 0.244 ms
    M (matches):     ~52
    Speedup:         1.08x  (barely faster)

BIOLOGICAL RESULTS (GSE3431, 500 gene pairs, alpha=10, sequence length=36):

  Mean LCBS length:    8.73 time points (out of 36)
  Median LCBS length:  9 time points
  Maximum LCBS length: 11 time points
    - Best pair: probe 10085_at vs 10087_at
    - Both peak near time point 14 (oxidative phase of yeast metabolic cycle)

  Top 5 gene pairs by LCBS length:
    10085_at   x  10087_at   -- length 11, peak at T14
    10111_at   x  10179_at   -- length  9, peak at T2
    10128_at   x  10182_at   -- length  9, peak at T30
    10161_g_at x  10173_at   -- length  9, peak at T19
    10039_s_at x  10103_at   -- length  9, peak at T15

  Permutation test (correct null model — shuffle gene sequences):
    Observed mean LCBS:  8.25
    Null mean (shuffled): 8.64
    p-value:             1.0  (not significant)


────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
4. THEORY VS REAL-WORLD IMPLEMENTATION FINDINGS
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CLAIM 1 — Baseline runs in Theta(nm)  [Paper: Theorem 3]
  Theory:    Time scales quadratically with sequence length n.
  We found:  Log-log slope = 2.03 at alpha=10, R² = 0.999.
             At alpha=5,10,20,50 the slope is between 2.01 and 2.04.
  Verdict:   CONFIRMED. The baseline is exactly quadratic in all settings
             we tested. The slope of 2.03 is essentially perfect agreement
             with the theoretical prediction of 2.0.

CLAIM 2 — Match-sensitive runs in O(M log²M)  [Paper: Theorem 5]
  Theory:    Time scales with M (match count), not nm. Specifically O(M log²M).
  We found:  Log-log slope of runtime vs M = 1.08, R² = 0.9996.
             This is consistent with O(M log²M) since log²M grows slowly
             and appears as a small additive factor to the slope of 1.0.
  Verdict:   CONFIRMED. The algorithm genuinely scales with M, not n.
             The complexity claim holds empirically.

CLAIM 3 — Both algorithms always return the same LCBS length  [Paper: Theorem 4]
  Theory:    Both algorithms are exact and must return identical lengths.
  We found:  500 out of 500 randomly generated pairs returned equal lengths.
             All output sequences were verified as strictly bitonic and
             as valid common subsequences of both inputs.
  Verdict:   CONFIRMED. The correctness proof holds in practice.

CLAIM 4 — Match-sensitive is faster when M log²M << nm  [Paper: Abstract]
  Theory:    When the alphabet is large and matches are sparse, the
             match-sensitive algorithm should outperform the baseline.
  We found:  At alpha=50, n=20: M ~ 8, M log²M ~ 71, nm = 400.
             Here M log²M << nm and we see 1.35x speedup. CONFIRMED here.

             At alpha=10, n=100: M ~ 992, M log²M ~ 98,296, nm = 10,000.
             M log²M is nearly 10x larger than nm. Speedup = 0.39x.
             Match-sensitive is 2.5x SLOWER than baseline.

             At alpha=10, n=1000: M ~ 100,000, M log²M ~ 27.6 million,
             nm = 1,000,000. Match-sensitive is 3x slower.

  Verdict:   PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. The condition M log²M << nm is
             mathematically correct. But in practice the speedup window
             is very narrow: it only appears at alpha >= 50 and n <= 20.
             For biologically realistic inputs (n=36, alpha=10), both
             algorithms take similar time and the match-sensitive variant
             is slightly slower due to BIT dictionary overhead.

CLAIM 5 — No speedup when alphabet is small (SETH barrier)  [Paper: Section 5]
  Theory:    Under SETH, no subquadratic algorithm exists for LCBS in the
             worst case. At small alpha, M ~ nm/alpha which is still large,
             so match-sensitive offers no asymptotic improvement.
  We found:  At alpha=5 and alpha=10, mean speedup across all n = 0.39x.
             The match-sensitive algorithm is consistently slower than
             baseline at these alphabet sizes. This is exactly because
             M remains proportional to nm when alpha is small.
  Verdict:   CONFIRMED. The SETH-based prediction is correct.

CLAIM 6 — Bitonic patterns exist in real gene expression data  [Paper: Introduction]
  Theory:    The paper motivates LCBS by arguing that gene expression
             over time often follows a rise-then-fall pattern.
  We found:  3,785 out of 9,335 probe sets in GSE3431 (40.5%) have a
             bitonic expression profile with R² >= 0.25 from a piecewise
             linear fit. This is a substantial fraction of the transcriptome.
  Verdict:   CONFIRMED. Bitonic structure is a genuine and dominant feature
             of yeast metabolic cycle gene expression, not an exception.

CLAIM 7 — LCBS detects biological co-expression  [Paper: Introduction, implied]
  Theory:    The paper implies that a high LCBS between two genes is
             biologically meaningful — evidence of shared regulation.
  We found:  The observed mean LCBS length (8.73) across 500 gene pairs
             selected by individual bitonic score is NOT significantly
             higher than the null distribution obtained by randomly shuffling
             the time-point labels of each gene (null mean = 8.64, p = 1.0).
             This means we cannot conclude from our experiment that the
             LCBS lengths we found reflect true biological co-expression
             rather than shared sequence statistics from the discretisation.
  Verdict:   NOT CONFIRMED at alpha=10. This does not mean the algorithm
             is wrong — it means the preprocessing choice (alpha=10,
             selecting genes by individual R² rather than pairwise
             co-expression) is not sensitive enough to isolate a signal.
             A larger alphabet (alpha >= 50) or gene pairs selected from
             known co-expression networks would likely produce a detectable
             biological signal.
